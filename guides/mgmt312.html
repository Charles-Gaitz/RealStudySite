  • All partners = agents of each other. Acts within scope bind the firm.

Chapter 31 — Bankruptcy

The largest chapter on the exam (covered over 2 days). Know the full process step-by-step, the priority of creditors, and exactly what can and cannot be discharged.

Chapter Summary

Bankruptcy law has two goals: (1) give the debtor a fresh start free from certain creditors’ claims, and (2) ensure equitable treatment of all creditors competing for limited assets. Federal bankruptcy courts have exclusive jurisdiction. Appeals go to federal district courts.

The Four Bankruptcy Chapters

ChapterNameWho Uses ItKey Feature
Ch 7Liquidation (Straight)Individuals & businessesAll nonexempt assets sold; debtor discharged of remaining debts. Most common.
Ch 11ReorganizationCorporations primarily; also individualsDebtor stays in business as Debtor in Possession (DIP); reorganization plan restructures debts.
Ch 12Family Farmers/FishermenFamily farmers & fishermenAdjustment of debts; person continues operations.
Ch 13Individual AdjustmentIndividuals with regular incomeRepayment plan over 3–5 years; debtor keeps assets.

Voluntary vs. Involuntary Petitions

Voluntary

Debtor files the petition. Must have received credit counseling within 180 days of filing. Filing itself constitutes an order for relief.

Involuntary

Creditors force the debtor into bankruptcy. 12+ creditors: need 3+ with unsecured claims totaling $18,600+. <12 creditors: 1+ creditor with $18,600+ claim. Court grants order for relief if debtor not paying debts as due, or custodian was appointed within 120 days before filing.

The Means Test HIGH YIELD

Used to determine eligibility for Ch 7. Compares debtor’s average income to the median family income in the debtor’s state.

  • Below median: Ch 7 is available.
  • Exceeds median by $6,000+: Abuse is presumed; trustee or creditor can move to dismiss.
  • In between: Further calculations required.
Texas median income (post May 2024): 1 person: $61,460 • 2: $79,870 • 3: $89,842 • 4: $108,866 • Add $9,900 per person over 4.

Automatic Stay HIGH YIELD

Effective the instant the petition is filed. Suspends almost all litigation and collection actions against the debtor. Willful violation = actual damages + costs + attorney fees.

Exceptions to Automatic Stay (these CAN continue):
  • Collection of domestic support obligations (child support, alimony)
  • Divorce, child custody/visitation, domestic violence proceedings
  • Investigations by a securities regulatory agency (SEC)
  • Certain government tax liens

Bad Faith: If 2+ petitions dismissed in the prior year → stay presumed in bad faith and does NOT go into effect automatically.

Bankruptcy Estate & Trustee

Bankruptcy Estate

Created at commencement of Ch 7. Includes ALL property held by debtor at filing + community property + proceeds. Filing date is the dividing line — property before filing = estate; property after = debtor’s.

Trustee’s Role

Collect estate, reduce to cash, distribute to creditors. Review materials for substantial abuse. File abuse statement within 10 days of first creditors’ meeting. Has strong-arm power (equivalent to judicial lien creditor’s rights).

Trustee’s Avoidance Powers HIGH YIELD

Preferences (90-Day Rule)

Trustee can recover payments made within 90 days before filing if: debtor was insolvent, the payment was for a pre-existing debt, and the creditor received more than in bankruptcy. Insiders (family, business partners): 1-year lookback.

Fraudulent Transfers (2-Year Rule)

Trustee can void transfers made within 2 years before filing with actual intent to hinder, delay, or defraud creditors. The key scenario: selling an asset for far below market value before filing.

Creditor Priority Order HIGH YIELD

1
Secured Creditors — Paid first from proceeds of their specific collateral. If collateral is insufficient, remainder becomes unsecured claim.
2
Domestic Support Obligations — Highest priority unsecured (child support, alimony, owed to spouse/former spouse/child/government unit).
3
Administrative Expenses — Costs of administering the bankruptcy estate.
4
Wages, Salaries, Commissions — Earned within 180 days before filing, up to $13,650 per person.
5
Government Taxes — Income taxes owed to federal and state governments.
6
General Unsecured Creditors — Paid pro-rata from what remains. In almost all Ch 7s, funds are insufficient.
7
Shareholders / Owners — Last in line. Receive anything only if all above classes are fully paid.
Each class must be fully paid before the next class receives anything. If funds run out mid-class, creditors in that class share pro-rata.

Discharge & Non-Dischargeable Debts HIGH YIELD

Discharge

Voids previous debts and prevents future collection actions. The debtor’s “fresh start.” Not all debts can be discharged.

Non-Dischargeable Debts (must know all of these):
  • Back taxes accruing within 2 years prior to bankruptcy
  • Domestic support (child support, alimony, property settlements)
  • Fraud — debts obtained by false pretenses or fraud
  • DUI liability — judgments from operating motor vehicle while intoxicated
  • Student loans — UNLESS undue hardship (very difficult standard to meet)
  • Willful/malicious conduct toward another person or their property
  • Luxury goods $800+ from a single creditor within 90 days of order for relief
  • Cash advances $1,100+ on open-end credit within 70 days of order for relief
Discharge Denied When (debtor’s misconduct): Concealment or destruction of property/records, prior discharge within 8 years, failure to complete consumer education course, involvement in proceedings where debtor could be found guilty of a felony. If denied, assets are still distributed to creditors but debtor remains liable for unpaid amounts.

Chapter 11 Reorganization (Key Points)

  • Debtor in Possession (DIP): Debtor continues to operate the business after order for relief. Court may appoint trustee if gross mismanagement shown.
  • Exclusive period: Only the debtor may file a plan within the first 120 days. Can extend but not beyond 18 months.
  • Plan acceptance: Majority of creditors in each class, representing two-thirds of the total claim amount, must vote to approve.
  • Cram-down: Court can confirm a plan even if only one class accepts it, as long as it is fair, equitable, and does not discriminate unfairly.
  • Workout: Private, negotiated adjustment between debtor and creditors — more flexible and cheaper than formal bankruptcy.

Exam Traps — Bankruptcy

Trap 1

The automatic stay does NOT stop domestic support collection, divorce proceedings, or SEC investigations. These continue.

Trap 2

The 90-day preference rule looks back from the filing date, not the order for relief date. For insiders, the lookback is 1 year.

Trap 3

Student loans are non-dischargeable UNLESS the debtor proves “undue hardship.” Courts apply this standard very strictly — merely being poor is not enough.

Trap 4

Discharge can be revoked within 1 year if the debtor acted fraudulently during the bankruptcy proceeding.

Trap 5

For voluntary petitions, the filing of the petition itself constitutes the order for relief — no separate court action needed.

Chapter 32 — Agency Formation & Duties

Agency is the fiduciary relationship where an agent acts on behalf of and subject to the control of a principal. One of the most pervasive legal relationships in business.

Chapter Summary

Agency = fiduciary relationship where principal controls agent. Any person can be an agent (even a minor). Only persons with contractual capacity can be principals. No writing or consideration required to form an agency. Created for any legal purpose.

Four Ways to Create an Agency HIGH YIELD

1
Agency by Agreement
Most common. Express written contract, oral agreement, or implied by conduct. Example: employer hires employee to handle sales.
2
Agency by Ratification
A non-agent acts on behalf of another, and the “principal” later approves (by words or conduct). Example: Abel buys $25K inventory for Paul (who only authorized $10K), Paul accepts and uses it → ratification binds Paul.
3
Agency by Estoppel
Principal’s actions create the appearance of agency; third party reasonably relies to their detriment. Created by PRINCIPAL’s actions, NOT the agent’s claims. Example: Company gives someone business cards and an office → third party relies on apparent authority → company estopped to deny agency.
4
Agency by Operation of Law
Courts find agency without formal agreement in: (1) Family relationships (spouse purchasing necessaries), (2) Emergency situations (agent cannot reach principal and failure to act would cause substantial loss).

Employee vs. Independent Contractor HIGH YIELD

Control Test — Most Important Factor

How much control does the employer exercise over the details of the work and day-to-day activities? More control = employee. Less control = independent contractor.

FactorPoints to EmployeePoints to IC
Control over work detailsHigh control by employerWorker controls own methods
Distinct occupation/businessSame business as employerDistinct from employer’s business
SupervisionUnder employer’s directionSpecialist, no supervision
Tools provided byEmployer provides tools & placeWorker provides own tools
Duration of employmentLong-term relationshipProject-based
Payment methodBy time period (weekly/monthly)By job completion
Skill requiredLow skill levelHigh specialized skill
Why it matters: Employees → employer must pay Social Security, workers’ comp, unemployment insurance, overtime (1.5x after 40 hrs/week), and employment discrimination laws apply. ICs → none of that. Also: work for hire — employee’s work product belongs to employer; IC keeps copyright unless agreement says otherwise.

Authority Types

Actual Authority

Express: Explicitly granted (written or oral). Implied: Reasonably necessary to carry out express authority (e.g., buying supplies to run the store).

Apparent Authority

Principal’s conduct causes third party to reasonably believe agent has authority. Principal is bound even if agent lacked actual authority. Third party’s reliance must be reasonable.

Agent’s Five Duties to Principal HIGH YIELD

Performance

Use reasonable diligence and skill. Must meet the skill level claimed. Gratuitous agents subject to tort (not contract) liability.

Notification

Notify principal of all relevant matters. Law assumes principal knows anything agent learns — regardless of whether agent actually told principal.

Loyalty

Act solely for principal’s benefit. No self-dealing. Confidentiality during AND after agency. Abel buys building for $1M, sells to Paul for $2M keeping the profit = breach of loyalty.

Obedience

Follow all lawful and clearly stated instructions. Deviation = breach (except in emergencies when principal cannot be reached).

Accounting

Keep accurate records of all property and funds. Must maintain SEPARATE accounts for principal’s funds. Cannot commingle with personal funds. Attorney violation also triggers disciplinary action.

PNLOA
Performance • Notification • Loyalty • Obedience • Accounting

Principal’s Four Duties to Agent

Compensation

Pay agreed-on value (or customary compensation if not specified) timely.

Reimbursement & Indemnification

Reimburse expenses incurred in reasonable performance. Indemnify for liabilities from authorized lawful acts.

Cooperation

Must cooperate and do nothing to prevent agent’s performance. Violating exclusive agency = liable for lost profits.

Safe Working Conditions

Inspect premises. Warn about unsafe conditions.

Exam Traps — Agency

Trap 1

Agency by agreement does NOT require writing or consideration. An oral agreement is valid.

Trap 2

The law imputes the agent’s knowledge to the principal whether or not the agent actually communicated it. If the agent knew, the principal is treated as knowing.

Trap 3

Agency by estoppel is created by the PRINCIPAL’s acts — not by the purported agent’s own claims. A person can’t make themselves an agent by simply claiming to be one.

Trap 4

Any person can be an agent (including minors). But only persons with contractual capacity can be principals.

Chapter 37 — Partnerships

A partnership is two or more persons carrying on, as co-owners, a business for profit. Governed by the Uniform Partnership Act (UPA) in the absence of an express agreement.

Essential Elements (3 Required) HIGH YIELD

Joint Ownership

Co-ownership of the business, not just property.

Sharing of Profits/Losses

Strongest indicator of partnership. If profits are shared, courts presume a partnership exists.

Equal Management Rights

Each partner has an equal right to participate in running the business.

Agreement can be oral, written, or implied by conduct. No formalities required. Corporation can be a partner. Cannot join a partnership without ALL existing partners’ consent.

UPA Default Rules (When No Agreement Exists) HIGH YIELD

IssueUPA Default Rule
ManagementEach partner has one vote, regardless of ownership percentage
ProfitsShared equally
LossesShared in same ratio as profits (equally if no profit agreement)
Ordinary decisionsMajority vote
Fundamental changesUnanimous vote required
CompensationPartner income = distribution of profits (no salary unless agreed)
BooksKept at principal business office; every partner has right to inspect and copy
TaxesPass-through entity — partnership files information return but pays no tax; income taxed to partners individually

Partners as Agents

Each partner is an agent of every other partner and of the partnership itself. Acts within the scope of the partnership business (or “business of the kind”) bind the firm. Every contract signed in the partnership’s name binds the firm. Partners also owe each other fiduciary duties.

Fiduciary Duties of Partners

Duty of Care

Limited to refraining from grossly negligent or reckless conduct, intentional misconduct, or knowing violation of law. NOT liable for simple negligence or honest errors in judgment.

Duty of Loyalty

Account for all property/profit/benefit derived from partnership business or property. Cannot compete with the partnership. Cannot self-deal. Cannot usurp a partnership opportunity. Cannot disclose trade secrets. Duties cannot be waived or eliminated in the partnership agreement.

Liability of Partners HIGH YIELD

Joint and Several Liability: Partners are personally liable for ALL partnership debts. Each partner can be held liable for the FULL amount of any partnership obligation. A plaintiff must sue all partners as a group, but can recover the full judgment from any single partner.
Apparent authority: When a partner carries on partnership business in the usual way, apparent authority exists and both the partner AND the firm share liability — even without actual authority.

Special Partnership Types

Limited Partnership (LP)

General partner(s): full management rights + full personal liability. Limited partner(s): contributes capital, no management right, liability limited to investment. General partner must exist.

Limited Liability Partnership (LLP)

Hybrid used by professionals (law firms, accounting firms). Pass-through tax. Partner’s personal liability for OTHER partners’ malpractice is limited.

Dissociation vs. Dissolution HIGH YIELD

Dissociation

One partner leaves the partnership. Terminates that partner’s actual authority and management rights. Partnership may CONTINUE without the dissociated partner. The partnership buys out the departing partner at the buyout price (what the partner would have received if firm terminated on that date).

Dissolution & Winding Up

The formal end of the partnership. Partners can only complete existing transactions and wind up. Cannot create new obligations. Assets distributed: pay creditors → return capital → distribute remainder per profit-sharing ratio.

Voting Requirements Summary

Majority Vote (Ordinary Matters)
  • Day-to-day business decisions
  • Hiring employees
  • Most operational decisions
Unanimous Vote (Fundamental Changes)
  • Admitting new partners
  • Amending the partnership agreement
  • Authorizing acts outside ordinary business
  • Any decision that significantly changes the nature of the partnership

Exam Traps — Partnerships

Trap 1

Duty of care is GROSS negligence standard — NOT ordinary negligence. A partner is not liable for honest errors in judgment or simple mistakes. This is MORE lenient than corporate duty of care.

Trap 2

Dissociation ≠ Dissolution. Dissociation = one partner leaves but firm continues. Dissolution = firm is wound down and terminated.

Trap 3

Even if a partner acts without authority, the partnership can still be bound if apparent authority exists (third party reasonably believed the partner had authority based on ordinary course of business).

Chapter 38 — Limited Liability Companies

The LLC is a hybrid entity combining limited liability of a corporation with pass-through taxation of a partnership. The preferred structure for many small businesses.

LLC Characteristics

Like a Corporation
  • Limited liability for members
  • Separate legal entity (can sue/be sued, hold property, enter contracts)
  • Must be formed and operated per state law
Like a Partnership
  • Pass-through taxation (default)
  • Flexible management structure
  • Operating agreement (like partnership agreement)

Formation

Articles of Organization

Filed with the secretary of state. Must include: name (must include “LLC” or “Limited Liability Company”), principal address, name/address of registered agent, members’ names, and how the LLC will be managed.

Management Structure HIGH YIELD

Member-Managed (Default)

All members participate in management. Decisions by majority vote. Default unless articles specify otherwise.

Manager-Managed

Members designate a manager (can be members, non-members, or mixed). Non-managing members have no management role.

Operating Agreement

Members set the details of how the business will be managed and operated. Not required in many states for the LLC to legally exist. Need not be in writing (but should be). Covers: profit distribution, management, voting, buyout provisions, dissolution procedures.

Default Rules When No Operating Agreement HIGH YIELD

IssueDefault Rule
ManagementMember-managed
Profit distributionSplit EQUALLY among members (NOT by capital contribution)
VotingMajority vote of members
TaxationPass-through (taxed as partnership)

Limited Liability & When It Can Be Lost

Normal rule: Members’ liability is limited to their investment. The LLC as an entity absorbs liability from members’ wrongful acts.
Liability can be imposed if:
  • Member personally guarantees a business loan
  • Member commingles personal and business funds → veil piercing

Dissociation from an LLC

Member ceases to be associated with carrying on the LLC business. Triggers: voluntary withdrawal, expulsion, court order, incompetence, bankruptcy, death.

After dissociation: Member loses management rights and agency authority. Duty of loyalty terminates. Duty of care continues for pre-dissociation events only. Member has right to buyout at fair value.

Winding Up & Distribution Order HIGH YIELD

1
Creditors paid first (including member-creditors owed money by the LLC)
2
Members’ capital contributions returned
3
Remaining amounts distributed equally (or per operating agreement)

Exam Traps — LLCs

Trap 1

Profits are split EQUALLY by default — NOT proportional to capital contributions. Capital contributions only matter for return of capital on dissolution.

Trap 2

After dissociation, duty of loyalty ENDS but duty of care CONTINUES for events that occurred before dissociation. Don’t confuse which duty continues.

Trap 3

Dissolution ≠ Dissociation. Dissociation = one member leaves. Dissolution = the entire LLC is wound up and terminated. A member’s dissociation does NOT automatically dissolve the LLC.

Chapter 39 — Corporate Formation & Financing

A corporation is a separate legal person created and recognized by state law. Limited liability for shareholders. Double taxation. Governed by state statutes (guided by RMBCA).

Key Characteristics

Advantages
  • Limited liability for shareholders
  • Perpetual existence (shareholders can change without affecting the corporation)
  • Can sue and be sued in its own name
  • Constitutional rights: due process, free speech, freedom from unreasonable search & seizure
Disadvantages
  • Double taxation: corporation pays tax on profits; shareholders pay tax on dividends
  • More complex and expensive to form and maintain
  • Must follow corporate formalities (or risk veil piercing)

Formation Process

Often the state where business will primarily operate
2

With secretary of state. Primary source of authority for the corporation. Includes: name, purpose, authorized shares, registered agent, incorporators.

Bylaws = internal rules of management. Cover voting requirements, how directors are elected and replaced.

Piercing the Corporate Veil HIGH YIELD

When courts pierce the veil:

When the corporate privilege is abused for personal benefit or when the corporate business is treated so carelessly it’s indistinguishable from the controlling shareholder’s personal affairs. Result: shareholders become personally liable for corporate debts.

Exam Traps — Corporations

Trap 1

“Public corporation” = government-owned (Amtrak, USPS). “Publicly held corporation” = traded on stock exchange. These are completely different concepts.

Trap 2

Double taxation: corporation pays tax on earnings, AND shareholders pay tax on dividends. This is the main disadvantage of the corporate form. LLCs avoid this with pass-through taxation.

Trap 3

Bonds are DEBT — bondholders are creditors, not owners. Bondholders get paid before stockholders in any dissolution.

Chapter 40 — Directors, Officers & Shareholders

The internal governance of a corporation. Board sets policy, officers execute, shareholders own but don’t manage. All fiduciaries owe duties of care and loyalty.

Directors vs. Officers HIGH YIELD

Board of Directors
  • Ultimate authority in every corporation
  • NOT agents — no individual director can bind the corporation alone
  • Policy decisions: selecting officers, declaring dividends, capital structure
  • Act as a BODY; each director gets one vote; majority rules
  • Elected by shareholders annually (or staggered terms)
  • Can be removed for cause; states differ on without-cause removal
Corporate Officers
  • Hired by the board of directors
  • ARE agents of the corporation
  • Run daily operations (CEO, CFO, VP, Secretary)
  • Rights defined by employment contracts
  • Board can remove any officer at any time with or without cause
  • Can hold multiple offices; can be both officer and director

Board Committees (Required for Public Companies)

Audit Committee

SOX requires: independent directors only + at least 1 member with finance/accounting expertise.

Compensation Committee

SEC & NYSE require independent directors only. Sets executive pay.

Nominating/Governance Committee

SEC & NYSE require independent directors only. Nominates new directors.

Directors’ Rights

Participation

Right to attend and vote at ALL board meetings. Must receive notice of meetings.

Inspection

Access to ALL books, records, facilities, premises. Almost absolute right — cannot be restricted by articles, bylaws, or board action.

Indemnification

Right to reimbursement of legal costs, fees, and damages incurred by virtue of position as director.

Duty of Care HIGH YIELD

Directors and officers must: (1) act in good faith, (2) exercise the care an ordinarily prudent person would exercise in similar circumstances, and (3) act in what they believe is the best interests of the corporation.

Duty to make informed decisions: Must investigate, study, discuss, and evaluate alternatives before deciding. Can rely on reports from: competent officers/employees, outside professionals, and board committees.

Business Judgment Rule (BJR) HIGH YIELD

Protection from liability for good-faith business decisions

Courts will NOT hold directors/officers liable for honest mistakes of judgment or bad business decisions IF all three conditions are met:

  • Took reasonable steps to become informed
  • Had a rational basis for the decision
  • No personal conflict between self-interest and corporation’s interest
BJR does NOT apply if: Bad faith, fraud, or clear breach of fiduciary duties. Courts consider the reasonableness of a decision at the TIME it was made — no benefit of hindsight.

Duty of Loyalty HIGH YIELD

Directors and officers must subordinate their personal interests to the welfare of the corporation. Key applications:

  • No competing with the corporation
  • Corporate opportunity doctrine: Cannot usurp a business opportunity that belongs to the corporation
  • No self-dealing: Cannot benefit personally from corporate transactions without full disclosure

Conflicts of Interest Procedure

1
Full disclosure
Director must disclose the nature of the conflicting interest
2
Disclose all pertinent facts
All information relevant to the transaction must be shared with the board
3
Abstain from voting
Conflicted director must NOT vote on the proposed transaction

Shareholder Rights & Powers

Shareholders Must Approve Fundamental Changes
  • Amend articles of incorporation or bylaws
  • Mergers
  • Dissolve the corporation
  • Sell all or substantially all corporate assets
  • Elect or remove directors
Voting Mechanics
  • 1 vote per share (common stock)
  • Quorum = 50%+ of outstanding shares present
  • Majority of shares present passes resolutions
  • Proxies allowed (shareholder appoints agent to vote)
  • Cumulative voting = minority shareholder protection

Shareholder Derivative Suit HIGH YIELD

What it is:

A suit brought by a shareholder to enforce a corporate cause of action when directors fail to act on the corporation’s behalf.

Process: (1) Shareholder must first submit a written demand to the board, asking them to take action. (2) If board refuses or fails to act, shareholder may file suit. Damages recovered go to the CORPORATION, not to the suing shareholder personally.

Exam Traps — Directors, Officers & Shareholders

Trap 1

Directors are NOT agents. A single director cannot bind the corporation. They must act as a group. Officers ARE agents and can bind the corporation individually.

Trap 2

In a derivative suit, the damages go to the CORPORATION, not to the shareholders personally, even though shareholders brought the suit.

Trap 3

BJR protects honest mistakes — but NOT if the director was uninformed, had a personal interest, or acted in bad faith. All three conditions must be met for protection.

Trap 4

Director inspection right is almost absolute — it cannot be restricted by the articles, bylaws, or any act of the board of directors.

Key Terms Master List

All major terms likely to be tested. Sorted alphabetically.

Agency
Fiduciary relationship where agent acts on behalf of and subject to control of principal.
Agency by Agreement
Agency created by express or implied consent between principal and agent. No writing required.
Agency by Estoppel
Created when principal’s conduct causes third party to reasonably believe agency exists; principal cannot deny it.
Agency by Ratification
Created when a non-agent acts on another’s behalf and the other later approves the act by words or conduct.
Articles of Incorporation
Document filed with the secretary of state to form a corporation. Primary source of corporate authority.
Articles of Organization
Document filed with the secretary of state to form an LLC.
Automatic Stay
Suspension of virtually all litigation and collection actions against the debtor, effective the moment bankruptcy is filed.
Bond
A debt security representing money borrowed by the corporation. Has a maturity date and fixed interest payments. Bondholders are creditors, not owners.
Business Judgment Rule
Courts will not hold directors/officers liable for good-faith business decisions if they were reasonably informed, had a rational basis, and had no personal conflict.
Bylaws
Internal rules of management adopted by a corporation at its first organizational meeting.
Chapter 7
Liquidation bankruptcy. Nonexempt assets sold by trustee; debtor discharged of remaining debts.
Chapter 11
Reorganization bankruptcy. Debtor continues as DIP; repayment plan restructures debts.
Chapter 12
Bankruptcy for family farmers and fishermen with regular income.
Chapter 13
Bankruptcy for individuals with regular income; 3–5 year repayment plan.
Close Corporation
Corporation with a small number of shareholders (often family members); no public trading market for shares.
Commingling
Mixing personal and business funds or property so they lose separate identity. Triggers veil piercing.
Common Stock
Equity security giving ownership in the corporation; 1 vote per share; last in line for dividends and dissolution.
Control Test
Used to determine employee vs. IC status. Most important factor: degree of employer control over details of work.
Corporate Opportunity Doctrine
Directors and officers cannot take for themselves a business opportunity that belongs to the corporation without first offering it to the corporation.
Cram-Down
Ch 11 provision allowing court to confirm a reorganization plan even if only one class of creditors accepted it, if the plan is fair and equitable.
Cumulative Voting
Voting method allowing minority shareholders to concentrate votes; total votes = shares x directors being elected; all votes can go to one candidate.
Debtor in Possession (DIP)
In Ch 11, the debtor who continues to operate the business after order for relief is entered.
Derivative Suit
A suit brought by a shareholder on behalf of the corporation when directors fail to act. Must first demand board action. Damages go to the corporation.
Discharge
Court order voiding a debtor’s debts and preventing future collection. Gives debtor a fresh start.
Dissociation
Departure of a member (LLC) or partner (partnership) from the entity. Entity may continue. Different from dissolution.
Dissolution
The formal disbanding and winding up of a business entity.
Dividend
Distribution of corporate profits to shareholders in proportion to shares held.
Domestic Corporation
A corporation organized under the law of the state where it does business.
Double Taxation
Corporate profits taxed at entity level; dividends paid to shareholders taxed again at personal level. Main disadvantage of corporations.
Duty of Care
Obligation of directors/officers to act in good faith, exercise ordinary prudence, and act in the corporation’s best interests.
Duty of Loyalty
Obligation to subordinate personal interests to the corporation’s welfare. No self-dealing, no usurping opportunities, no competing.
Estate in Bankruptcy
All property interests of the debtor at the time of filing; created at commencement of Ch 7.
Fiduciary
A person with duty to act primarily for another’s benefit; relationship founded on trust and confidence.
Foreign Corporation
A corporation doing business in a state where it is not incorporated.
Fraudulent Transfer
Transfer made within 2 years of filing with intent to hinder, delay, or defraud creditors. Trustee can void it.
Independent Contractor (IC)
Worker whose working conditions and methods are NOT controlled by the employer. Not subject to employment laws.
Joint Liability
All partners can be held liable for the full amount of a partnership obligation.
LLC
Limited Liability Company. Hybrid entity with limited liability (like a corp) and pass-through taxation (like a partnership).
Means Test
Test comparing debtor’s income to state median to determine eligibility for Ch 7. Income $6,000+ above median = abuse presumed.
Operating Agreement
Agreement among LLC members governing management, operations, profit distribution, and dissolution. Not required but strongly recommended in writing.
Order for Relief
Court’s grant of bankruptcy protection. For voluntary petitions, the filing itself constitutes the order.
Partnership
An agreement by 2+ persons to carry on, as co-owners, a business for profit.
Pass-Through Entity
Business entity with no tax liability; income passes through to owners who pay personal tax. Partnerships, LLCs, S-Corps.
Preference (Bankruptcy)
Payment to one creditor within 90 days of filing that gave that creditor more than they would have received in bankruptcy. Trustee can recover it.
Preferred Stock
Equity security with fixed dividend priority over common stock. Usually no voting rights. Priority over common stock on dissolution.
Principal
The party in an agency relationship who authorizes the agent to act on their behalf. Must have contractual capacity.
Proxy
Authorization for someone else to vote a shareholder’s shares at a corporate meeting.
Ratification
A principal’s approval of an unauthorized act by an agent or non-agent, which creates a binding agency relationship retroactively.
S Corporation
A close corporation taxed as a partnership under the IRS code. Max 100 shareholders; domestic only; no affiliated group members.
Strong-Arm Power
Trustee’s power to act as a judicial lien creditor against the debtor’s property; allows trustee to void certain transfers.
UPA
Uniform Partnership Act. Governs operation of partnerships in the absence of an express partnership agreement.
Veil Piercing
Court action disregarding the corporate (or LLC) entity and holding shareholders/members personally liable for entity debts.
Workout
Private, negotiated debt adjustment between debtor and creditors to avoid formal bankruptcy. More flexible and cheaper.
Winding Up
Second stage of termination: collecting and liquidating assets, paying liabilities, and distributing remainder.
